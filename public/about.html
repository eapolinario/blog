About Liminal Desiderata

This blog explores the thresholds between systems design and human judgment, between engineering tradeoffs and values, between what exists and what is desired but not yet realized.

What This Is

Liminal Desiderata is a collection of technical essays and personal philosophy aimed at experienced engineers, technical leaders, and reflective practitioners. The writing assumes familiarity with software systems, tradeoffs, and abstraction.

Topics include:

  • Distributed systems and infrastructure design
  • Abstraction, invariants, and failure modes
  • Career decisions and technical leadership
  • Tooling, productivity, and taste
  • Decision-making under uncertainty
  • Values encoded in systems

The Name

Liminal comes from the Latin līmen (“threshold”). It refers to transitional or in-between states—periods where invariants are suspended and moments before structure hardens into decisions.

Desiderata is the plural of /desideratum/—“things desired but not yet attained.” These are articulated desired properties or outcomes that guide tradeoff discussions rather than end them.

Together, Liminal Desiderata captures the named things we want while we are between stable states.

Approach

The writing here:

  • Connects technical specifics to broader judgments
  • Names tradeoffs explicitly rather than hiding them
  • Assumes technical sophistication in readers
  • Optimizes for long-term intellectual coherence, not virality
  • Treats design and life decisions as structurally similar problems

About the Author

Eduardo Apolinario is a software engineer interested in distributed systems, infrastructure, and the human aspects of technical decision-making.
